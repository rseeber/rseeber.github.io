Several states have sued TikTok for addictive features, and damaging youth mental health

October 8, 2024

Just today, several states have filed lawsuits in state courts against TikTok, alleging harm to youth, according to The Associated Press. Texas is suing specifically regarding their new law against data-collection of minors online. States specifically complain about the ability to endlessly scroll videos for hours on end, and default enabled push notifications that try to bring you back to the app while you're away. In addition, they reference the face filters on the app, which create unattainable standards of appearance for these youth in the videos they see.

This comes following the rulings in August by the federal government against google, which I discussed in my last post. It can be seen as another step forward as the government continues to actually punish companies who break laws or are otherwise actively harming internet users.

2017 paper argues that counterspeech doesn't work online

September 15, 2024

Recently I read a paper about the issues of fake news online. It was written in 2017 following the US Presidential Election of 2016, with a major theme being investigating why fake news was so successful on this mostly untested new platform for the dissemination of information. The author argued that, in the online social media landscape, simply advocating for counter speech no longer works to win against bad ideas. That is, our first amendment principle of more speech being the solution to bad speech (such as false claims, bad ideas, hate speech, etc) only works under certain conditions, and those conditions are not met in our current social media ecosystem.

The author took the stance that social media platforms have a democratic duty to fix the conditions such that good speech is able to win out against bad speech again – essentially fix the rules to actually incentivise truthful claims so they can win out in the marketplace of ideas online. They also take a kind of position that some of the work might also fall on government to enforce this kind of thing as well.

Personally, I think a major point missed perhaps was the glaring fact that we as a culture are putting far too much stock in information that we find online on social media. By moving away from a social media obsessed viewpoint, we can instead reason and debate in the real world, where the counterspeech works just fine. The issue is that society has a false belief that good ideas come from social media at all. Though perhaps the issue goes a bit deeper than that. After all, it might be a little radical to expect the whole world to make a push away from social media after adopting it so heavily.

New paper about Noncomplience with Cybersecurity standards in Universities and Colleges of USA

September 11, 2024

I recently read a research paper over on Arxiv.org about cybersecurity in higher education. The researches found that nearly all higher education institutions are heavily lagging behind in updating their cybersecurity policies as new standards are released by NIST. The paper, published August 2024, found that even high level, prestigious universities were not up to date with standards even after having nearly 8 years to adjust since they were released in 2016.

Personally, I find this worrying because of the large number of other nodes that most college email accounts have access to. The fact that even the more prestigious colleges in the US are failing on multiple levels in their stated cybersecurity policies should raise alarm bells for everyone, especially for college students. That's not even to mention the kind of internal issues that institutions are susceptible to due to these failures of policy.
